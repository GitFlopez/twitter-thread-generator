# Example: IT Director Hook Thread + Story Thread

**Profile:** Marcus Rivera, IT Director (15 years experience), Las Vegas, transitioning to CISO  
**Goal:** Build personal brand, attract CISO opportunities, grow tech leadership audience

---

## Hook Thread Example (Prompt 1 Output)

**Input:**
- Topic: Managing enterprise IT teams remotely
- Angle: Most remote IT failures are communication failures, not technical failures
- Audience: CIOs, CTOs, IT Directors
- Goal: Followers + inbound CISO inquiries
- Voice: Direct, experienced, no fluff

---

**Tweet 1:** I managed a 200-person remote IT team for 3 years across 4 time zones. Here are 11 things I wish someone told me before I started: 🧵 [108 chars]

**Tweet 2:** 1/ Your best engineers will leave if you don't give them visibility. Remote work makes quiet contributors invisible. Build a weekly "wins" channel in Slack — not optional. [171 chars]

**Tweet 3:** 2/ Stand-ups don't work for async teams. Replace them with a 3-question Friday check-in: What shipped? What's blocked? What do I need Monday morning? Takes 5 minutes. [167 chars]

**Tweet 4:** 3/ On-call burnout is silent. Most engineers won't complain until they have an offer letter in hand. Rotate on-call every sprint. Cap pages at 3 per night. Track escalation fatigue monthly. [191 chars]

**Tweet 5:** 4/ Documentation is the async team's oxygen. If it's not written down, it doesn't exist. I required every runbook to pass a "stranger test" — could a new hire execute it without asking anyone? [194 chars]

**Tweet 6:** 5/ Trust metrics over feelings. "I haven't heard from them" is not a performance issue. Ship velocity, incident response time, and ticket closure rate tell you everything. [169 chars]

**Tweet 7:** 6/ The first 90 days for a remote hire are make-or-break. If they don't have a buddy, a clear project, and a 30-day check-in, you're gambling with a $150K hire. [161 chars]

**Tweet 8:** 7/ Security incidents don't sleep in your timezone. Build an incident response playbook before you need it — not at 2 AM when production is down. I learned this the hard way. [175 chars]

**Tweet 9:** 8/ Skip-level 1:1s every quarter. Your managers will protect you from uncomfortable truths. Your ICs won't. Two skip-levels surfaced problems I didn't know I had. [163 chars]

**Tweet 10:** 9/ The camera-off debate is a trap. Some people focus better without it. Some teams feel disconnected without it. Decide together, write it down, and stop relitigating it every Monday. [186 chars]

**Tweet 11:** 10/ Promotion decisions hit differently when nobody can see each other working. Document your criteria in writing before the cycle, not after someone gets passed over. [166 chars]

**Tweet 12:** 11/ The biggest remote IT failure I ever saw: a 40-person team that never had a shared Slack channel for non-work conversation. High performers left in month 4. Build belonging intentionally. [191 chars]

**Tweet 13:** If you're leading an IT team remotely and any of this landed — follow me. I share frameworks, war stories, and hard-won lessons from 15 years in enterprise IT. DM me "REMOTE" if you want the full playbook. [208 chars]

**Hook Score: 9/10** — Number + "wish someone told me" formula creates instant authority + relatability. The "3 years + 4 time zones" specificity signals real experience.

**Engagement Tips:**
- Post Tuesday or Wednesday, 7-8 AM Pacific
- Reply to every comment in the first hour
- Pin this thread to your profile when it performs

**Repurposing Kit (brief):**

## Story Thread Example (Prompt 4 Output)

**Input:**
- Story: A major security incident that almost ended my career — and what I learned about incident response
- Lesson: The plan matters less than the team you've built
- Audience: IT leaders, security professionals
- Voice: 3/5 (candid, not raw)

---

**Tweet 1:** In 2021, we had a ransomware incident at 2:47 AM on a Saturday. 3,000 endpoints. 47 minutes to full containment. Here's the story — and why I got lucky: 🧵 [156 chars]

**Tweet 2:** I'd been IT Director for 18 months. We had a response plan. We'd even run a tabletop drill three months earlier. I thought we were ready. We weren't. [150 chars]

**Tweet 3:** The call came from our NOC. "Marcus, we're seeing lateral movement on the domain." My stomach dropped. I knew what that meant. I pulled up the dashboard. It was worse than I thought. [184 chars]

**Tweet 4:** My plan said: isolate, document, escalate, communicate. What actually happened: the three engineers on overnight rotation self-organized before I even got on the bridge call. [175 chars]

**Tweet 5:** They'd run our tabletop drill together. They knew each other's instincts. While I was still reading the situation, they'd already isolated the first 400 endpoints. I was behind my own team. [191 chars]

**Tweet 6:** 47 minutes later, we had 99% containment. Cleanup took two weeks. But the blast radius was 10% of what it should have been. Not because of my plan. Because of those three engineers. [183 chars]

**Tweet 7:** The CISO of our parent company asked me how we contained it so fast. I said: "Honestly? I built a team that didn't need me in the first hour." He asked if I'd ever considered moving into security leadership. [208 chars]

**Tweet 8:** What I learned: Plans don't stop incidents. People do. The tabletop drill wasn't worth anything because of the slides — it was worth something because it put the right people in a room together. [196 chars]

**Tweet 9:** If you're leading an IT or security team: run drills. Not because your plan is good. Because your people need to know each other's instincts before everything goes sideways. [174 chars]

**Tweet 10:** If any of this resonated — follow me. I share what 15 years of enterprise IT actually looks like (the wins and the near-misses). DM me if you want to compare incident response notes. [184 chars]

**Hook Score: 9/10** — Specific timestamp (2:47 AM), specific scale (3,000 endpoints, 47 minutes), and the phrase "I got lucky" creates immediate tension. Reader wants to know what lucky means.
